To whom it may concern

by Ronnel Bermas
       
       I've heard that the "gift" lies within man—that to use it means to break one from the outside and induce some inward transformation of sorts. And if such were true, I need not ask if I am suffering anymore; I need to ask whether I am doing it enough.

       But I don't want the gifts; I want to be in the middle of mediocrity and greatness—where the landscape is stable and is rejoicing with balance. There, the waves would greet my feet like a long-lost friend and guide them back to their home.

       The sand will foam, bubbles forming as the shore hugs my body halfway; my skin, gradually getting accustomed to the unfamiliar touch of light.

       "I've come to ask which is better—the dampness of the ocean or the barrenness of land?"¹

       Yet perhaps I'm not searching for answers, but someone—to reach for my hand and save me from drowning. Because for humans, to consume more water than needed is to remove life out of their lungs. To lose another's touch is to lose all the warmth.

       I want to lose control and be reborn with trust alone, for only with trust do we dance in unison with uncertainty even if it means we're hanging onto our last lifelines. As I sink, the salt will fill the spaces fragmenting my skin with more colors than one could ever behold. Then, you will see—my wounds will bathe on your tempest and glimmer brighter than they have ever stung.²

       Once more, I will submit myself towards the intersection where there is neither strength nor weakness, but only truth.³

*

¹What is God doing on a cross, if not to meet us in our tears?

²My flesh will open to a world where understanding meets suffering; I’ll schedule the windows daily to depict how
someone could have been our mirror image. We could meet there to see if we’re exactly alike.

³And only then do we understand.
