Orange juice

by Jenelle Capati       

       When there was sun, there was morning.

       Bright enough to marry the streets in a glow, where our playground would sit steadily in the distance. My lanky feet would run towards that happiness, and I would meet all of them there. Smiles, rocks, and swings. It was then I learned that when there was sun, it was also noon.

       And perhaps that’s a fact.

       But when they stayed overnight, bringing freshly-cut flowers, bickering endlessly with a mouthful of chips, and turning the pegs of an offish violin—all under the comforts of a dimly-lit living room and a pillow fort. It was then I learned that when there was sun, it was also evening.

       And perhaps, I was naive it could only ever be that way.

       That the morning will be filled with sun. That the noon will be filled with sun. That the evening will always, always—be filled with sun.

      And when it can’t anymore, I had to look someplace else. Suddenly, the want is a need and I’ll be running out. That the endless days of light will slowly bleed into the starless dusk. That if I dare open my eyes to another morning, I would forget everything before me. And leave it all behind.

      But when I did look behind me, I’ve already cast a shadow against the orange pavement.

      I move my arm, the shadow moves. I run farther away, the shadow stretches taller. It was then I learned that when there was sun, it was also sunset. And inevitably—


When there was sun, there was mourning.
